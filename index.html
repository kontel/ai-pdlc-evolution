The AI-Augmented PDLC

A structural evolution of enterprise delivery—full diagram opens in a lightbox.

Executive Summary

Augmenting Agile, Not Abandoning It

AI tools are reshaping how enterprise teams plan, estimate, build, and deliver software. Forrester confirms that 95% of professionals still affirm agile's relevance—but the overhead must go.

Key Finding — McKinsey, Nov 2025

Top-performing organizations see shorter sprint cycles, smaller team sizes, and higher customer satisfaction—but only when they rethink team structures and processes around AI, not just bolt tools onto existing workflows. Individual productivity gains average 20–50%, yet company-wide improvements only reach 5–15% without structural change.

The Evolution Map: Legacy → AI-Augmented

Phase-by-phase outline of how each major PDLC component can evolve. Designed for incremental adoption.

Phase 1 · Months 1–3

AI-Assisted Planning

AI pre-populates sprint plans based on historical velocity, team capacity (accounting for PTO), and backlog priorities. The European Scrum AI Guide documents teams implementing systems that analyze the last 30 sprints to generate alerts on blocking potential and parallel clusters. Planning poker shrinks from hours to minutes with AI-provided baseline estimates.

Phase 2 · Months 3–6

Hybrid Sprint/Kanban

Maintain sprint cadence for human-led strategic work while running AI-suitable tasks (boilerplate, standard integrations, test generation) in a continuous Kanban flow. Real-time adaptive workflows let AI continuously monitor progress, flag bottlenecks, and recommend reallocation.

Phase 3 · Months 6–12

Continuous Planning

Sprint planning as a distinct ceremony largely dissolves. AI maintains real-time capacity, priority, and risk views. Planning meetings become short alignment checks (15–30 minutes). Team energy shifts from process mechanics to value-creation decisions.

Phase 1 · Months 1–3

AI-Augmented Story Points

AI analyzes past commits, pull requests, and issue resolution times to suggest story-point estimates. Teams validate, anchoring on data rather than guesswork. Teams begin separating "AI-suitable" from "AI-resistant" tasks—a project might see major time reduction on CRUD but none on UX design.

Phase 2 · Months 3–6

Recalibrate or Abandon Points

As AI shifts team velocity dramatically (some Scrum.org practitioners report 3–5x increases), historical baselines become meaningless. Two paths: recalibrate or shift to throughput-based metrics—items delivered per sprint and cycle time. The community increasingly recommends the latter.

Phase 3 · Months 6–12

Outcome-Based Metrics

Shift from output metrics (velocity, points) to outcome metrics (cycle time, lead time, deployment frequency, customer satisfaction). A three-tiered framework emerges: "Zero-Point" for fully AI-automated tasks, "Standard" for human-led work, and "Review & Integration" for validating AI output.

Phase 1 · Quarter 1

AI-Augmented PI Planning

AI pre-builds dependency maps from source code, task histories, and architectural diagrams. Backlog items arrive pre-estimated, pre-prioritized, with AI-generated stories. The 2-day event compresses to 1 day. AI-powered capacity forecasting eliminates "confidence vote" guesswork.

Phase 2 · Quarters 2–3

Monthly Strategy Syncs

Replace the quarterly big-room event with monthly lightweight strategy syncs plus always-on AI monitoring. Rolling forecasts recalibrate weekly based on real delivery data. As McKinsey's research emphasizes: organizations developing strategies iteratively are growing faster than those planning in rigid quarterly cycles.

Phase 3 · Quarters 3–4

Continuous Strategic Alignment

The quarterly ceremony dissolves into continuous practice. AI dashboards show real-time feature-to-objective alignment. Risk assessment and cross-team dependency resolution are proactive. The formal event becomes an optional half-day "check and adjust."

Traditional quarterly planning doesn't make sense when you can test and validate an idea in a week. The teams succeeding are the ones with the best decision-making velocity.

Spec-Driven Development

Investment shifts from writing code to writing precise specifications. GitHub released Spec Kit (Sep 2025) for this approach. Product managers iterate specs with AI agents, engineers become architects and orchestrators. If AI output isn't right, you refine the spec and regenerate—iteration is low-cost.

Developer as Product Owner

With coding capacity no longer the bottleneck, the constraint shifts to figuring out what to build. Developers increasingly make product judgment calls—what's worth building, what solves user problems, and what's just a technical flex. This requires user empathy and business understanding.

Team Topologies for AI-Augmented Delivery

The pod model becomes more actionable when paired with Team Topologies. Instead of one generic team shape, enterprises are likely to evolve toward stream-aligned product teams supported by platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem teams, with AI changing the balance of work inside each topology.

Stream-Aligned Teams

These become the primary product pods, aligned to a customer journey or business domain. AI agents sit inside the team as accelerators, but the team still owns outcomes, trade-offs, and the four product risks for its area.

Platform Teams

Platform teams evolve from shared services into builders of self-service AI guardrails: golden paths, evaluation harnesses, workflow templates, secure model access, and compliant delivery pipelines that reduce cognitive load on product teams.

Enabling Teams

Short-lived enabling teams help product teams adopt prompt patterns, spec-driven development, AI review practices, discovery methods, and model governance. Their job is capability transfer, not becoming a permanent approval layer.

Complicated-Subsystem Teams

Highly specialized domains—pricing engines, risk models, ML systems, legacy cores, regulated integrations—may remain dedicated expert teams. AI helps them move faster, but deep domain expertise and tighter controls still justify specialization.

Interaction Modes Matter

Team Topologies also sharpens how teams work together. Expect collaboration during product discovery and architecture spikes, X-as-a-service consumption from platform teams once patterns stabilize, and facilitating interactions from enabling teams during adoption waves. In practice, AI increases the need for good boundaries and better interfaces between teams, not fewer of them.

The Engineering Discipline: Harnesses, Loops, and Quality

Practitioner research from Thoughtworks' "Exploring Gen AI" series on martinfowler.com (Jan–Mar 2026) provides the critical framework for AI-augmented engineering.

5.1 Humans "On the Loop", Not In or Out

Kief Morris (Thoughtworks, Mar 2026) introduces a framework for where humans belong in AI-augmented development. He distinguishes the "why loop" (what to build) from the "how loop" (mechanics of building), then identifies three positions:

Outside the Loop

"Vibe Coding"

Humans define the outcome, agents handle everything else. Appealing because the "why loop" is what we care about. But in practice, agents produce messy codebases that spiral over time. Internal quality still matters because clean structure makes agents work faster.

In the Loop

Line-by-line review

Developers inspect every line of AI output. The problem: humans become a bottleneck. Agents generate code faster than humans can review. This may explain why some productivity reports show mixed results—review time offsets generation speed.

Recommended

On the Loop

Building the harness

Rather than inspecting individual output, engineers build and maintain the system that makes agents produce better output—the "harness." When output is unsatisfactory, improve the harness, not the individual artifact. This is the key insight: the job shifts from writing code to engineering the system that writes code.

The Agentic Flywheel

Morris describes directing agents to manage and improve the harness itself. Feed agents rich signals—test results, pipeline metrics, production data, user journey logs—and have them recommend harness improvements. Start reviewing recommendations interactively; as confidence grows, let high-scoring recommendations be auto-applied. This is how you get a self-improving development system.

5.2 Harness Engineering: The New Core Discipline updated 🔗

Birgitta Böckeler (Thoughtworks, Feb 2026) coins "harness engineering" as the practice of building tooling and constraints that keep AI agents in check. The term comes from OpenAI's write-up on using Codex to build a 1-million-line application with zero manually typed code—after 5 months of harness-building.

Context Engineering

A continuously enhanced knowledge base in the codebase, plus agent access to dynamic context like observability data and browser navigation.

Architectural Constraints

Monitored by LLM-based agents and deterministic custom linters and structural tests (like ArchUnit). Specific patterns, enforced boundaries, standardized structures.

"Garbage Collection" Agents

Agents that run periodically to find inconsistencies in documentation or violations of architectural constraints, fighting entropy and decay.

Key Insight

Böckeler envisions harnesses as the new service templates. Just as most organizations have two or three main tech stacks, teams will pick from a set of harnesses for common application topologies. Counterintuitively, increasing AI autonomy requires constraining the solution space—you give up "generate anything" flexibility to get reliability.

Research — arXiv, Mar 2026

A March 2026 arXiv paper, "Building AI Coding Agents for the Terminal: Scaffolding, Harness, Context Engineering, and Lessons Learned", formalises harness engineering as a three-layer discipline for terminal-based AI coding agents:

  • Input filtering — constraining what the agent sees (file scope, token budgets, retrieval strategies) to prevent hallucination from irrelevant context.
  • Output validation — deterministic gates (type checks, linters, security scanners, test suites) that reject agent output before it reaches the codebase.
  • Permission & observability — audit trails, approval workflows, and real-time dashboards so humans can monitor agent behaviour without blocking it.

The paper notes that OpenAI reportedly shipped an internal product with 1 M+ lines of code and zero manually written lines using Codex under strict harness constraints—reinforcing Böckeler's thesis that harness investment is a prerequisite, not a luxury. Emerging guides from NxCode (2026) and Eric Mann further argue that traditional code reviews alone are insufficient—agents need purpose-built guardrails that operate at a structural level.

5.3 Context Engineering: The New Skill

In a companion piece (Feb 2026), Böckeler maps how we configure what a coding agent sees. Her practical insights:

Build context iteratively

Don't front-load massive rules files. Models are powerful enough that what you needed to specify six months ago may no longer be necessary. Add guidance gradually and validate that each addition actually improves results.

Sharing is harder than it looks

Context works much better within a team than between strangers. Different experience levels need different rules. Blindly copying configurations leads to contradictory instructions.

Beware the illusion of control

Context engineering is not deterministic. People say "ensure it does X" but with LLMs, we must think in probabilities and choose the right level of human oversight for the job.

5.4 The Internal Quality Problem

Erik Doernenburg (Thoughtworks, Jan 2026) documents a case study implementing a GitLab integration using AI agents. His findings are a direct caution against over-trusting AI output:

Subtle type-system degradation

The agent declared all function parameters as non-optional when they should have been optional. When this caused errors, the agent's fix was inserting empty-string defaults everywhere—bypassing Swift's type safety. The correct fix was a single character change.

Unnecessary complexity

The agent introduced a completely unnecessary cache, implemented complicated logic for a non-existent problem, and "forgot" to reuse existing functions, duplicating logic in multiple places.

Confabulation under pressure

When the API required a separate call for user avatar data, the agent insisted the data was available in the main response, confusing documentation from multiple endpoints.

The generated code worked. It implemented the functionality required. But it would've added completely unnecessary complexity and missed non-obvious functionality. Without careful oversight, agents seem to have a strong tendency to introduce technical debt.

— Erik Doernenburg, Thoughtworks, Jan 2026

5.5 Comprehension Debt: The Hidden Cost Beyond Technical Debt new 🔗

Addy Osmani (Mar 2026) names a risk distinct from traditional technical debt: comprehension debt—the growing gap between how much code exists in a system and how much of it any human genuinely understands. Unlike tech debt, it accumulates invisibly; the codebase appears healthy while understanding quietly erodes underneath.

A junior engineer can now generate code faster than a senior engineer can critically audit it. When AI-generated code is running in healthcare systems, financial infrastructure, and government services, “the AI wrote it and we didn’t fully review it” will not hold up in a post-incident report.

— Addy Osmani, Mar 2026

The Speed Asymmetry Problem

AI generates code faster than humans can evaluate it, breaking historical review dynamics. An Anthropic study of 52 engineers found the AI-assisted group scored 17% lower on comprehension quizzes (50% vs. 67%), with the largest declines in debugging performance.

17% lower comprehension — Anthropic study

The Measurement Gap

Velocity metrics, DORA metrics, and code coverage all remain “immaculate” while comprehension deficits accumulate invisibly. Teams feel productive but are building on foundations they cannot reason about. Developers using passive delegation (“just make it work”) scored below 40% on comprehension tests.

Below 40% for passive delegation

Why Common Defences Fall Short

Osmani argues that leaning on tests cannot catch unspecified behaviours; detailed-enough specs become the program itself (a waterfall fallacy); and code review volume overwhelms reviewers—surface cleanliness triggers false merge confidence.

“The comprehension work IS the job”

The 90-Day Degradation Curve

Research from Exceeds AI (Feb 2026) tracking AI-augmented teams over 90 days found rework rates rising 30–41%, incident rates per PR climbing to 35%, and code complexity growing 4.9×. AI-generated code shows 1.7× more issues than human-authored code overall.

The “Vibe, Then Verify” Response — Sonar, Feb 2026

Sonar's developer survey (88% of developers report at least one negative AI impact on tech debt) proposes a dual-mode discipline: Vibe—grant developers freedom to experiment boldly with AI; Verify—enforce accountability through deterministic, rules-based validation (static analysis, architectural linters, security scanners). The shift is not less AI, but better harnesses around it. Notably, developers spend 23–25% of their work week on toil regardless of AI usage—the toil simply changes shape, from writing boilerplate to reviewing and correcting AI output.

The 18-Month Collapse Pattern — Codebridge, Feb 2026

Without governance, AI-augmented projects follow a predictable arc: Months 1–3, visible feature acceleration; Months 4–9, velocity plateaus, integration delays emerge; Months 10–15, review and debug time becomes the bottleneck; Months 16–18, delivery cycles stall entirely. By year two, maintenance costs reach 4× traditional levels. Gartner projects 40% of AI-augmented coding projects will face cancellation by 2027.

How Non-Coding Tasks Are Evolving

Today · AI-Assisted

Infrastructure as Code generation

AI agents generate Terraform, CloudFormation, and Kubernetes configs from natural language descriptions. CI/CD pipelines are auto-configured based on project structure. Monitoring dashboards auto-generated from service topology. AWS announced dedicated DevOps agents at re:Invent 2025, and tools like Pulumi have integrated AI-driven infrastructure generation.

Near-term · Agentic

Self-healing infrastructure

AIOps platforms detect anomalies and automatically trigger remediation runbooks. Incident diagnosis agents correlate logs, metrics, and traces to identify root causes before humans are paged. The goal is "ticketless operations"—problems detected and resolved before users notice.

Future · Autonomous

Continuous environment orchestration

AI agents manage environment provisioning end-to-end: spin up preview environments per PR, auto-scale based on predicted load, decomission unused resources. The developer's role shifts from writing infra configs to defining intent and reviewing cost/performance tradeoffs.

Today · AI-Assisted

Smart triage and auto-routing

AI classifies incoming tickets by type, priority, and team. Duplicate detection prevents redundant work. ServiceNow, Jira, and similar platforms now embed AI for auto-categorization and suggested resolutions. Change request templates are auto-populated from code diffs.

Near-term · Agentic

End-to-end ticket lifecycle

AI agents create tickets from failing tests or production alerts, link them to relevant code changes, suggest fix PRs, and auto-close when the fix is deployed and verified. Change Advisory Board (CAB) reviews get AI-generated risk assessments with blast-radius analysis.

Future · Autonomous

Ticketless operations

For routine categories, the ticket is created, actioned, and resolved without human intervention. Humans are only pulled in for novel or high-risk changes. The ticket system becomes an audit log rather than a work queue.

Today · AI-Assisted

Codebase and API exploration

AI agents can navigate unfamiliar codebases, explain architectural patterns, trace data flows, and summarize API documentation. Developers use AI to understand legacy systems in hours instead of weeks. MCP (Model Context Protocol) enables agents to directly query live services and databases for exploration.

Near-term · Agentic

Automated impact analysis

Before starting a story, AI agents map the blast radius: which services are affected, what tests exist, what dependencies will break. They query service registries, read runbooks, and compile a "readiness brief" that replaces hours of manual research and exploration.

Future · Autonomous

Knowledge synthesis agents

Always-on agents that maintain living documentation of system architecture, API contracts, and institutional knowledge. When a developer asks "how does payment processing work?", the agent synthesizes from code, docs, Slack history, and production telemetry—not from a stale wiki page.

Today · AI-Assisted

Meeting prep and follow-up

AI generates meeting agendas from ticket context, produces structured summaries with action items, and drafts follow-up messages. Before an SME conversation, AI compiles relevant context: recent changes to the system, open questions from tickets, and suggested talking points.

Near-term · Agentic

Dependency negotiation support

AI agents identify cross-team dependencies from code and ticket data, draft proposals for API contract changes, and facilitate asynchronous coordination. Multi-agent protocols (like the A2A framework launched by Linux Foundation, Jun 2025, with Adobe, Microsoft, SAP, ServiceNow) enable agent-to-agent capability discovery.

Remains · AI-Resistant

Trust-building and judgment calls

The irreducibly human core: negotiating priorities with stakeholders, building trust with SMEs who hold tribal knowledge, navigating organizational politics, and making product judgment calls that require empathy and context that can't be captured in any prompt. AI can prepare you, but can't replace you in the room.

The Implication

When organizations evaluate AI impact, they often measure only coding productivity. But coding is frequently less than half of a story's effort. Teams that only adopt coding assistants capture a fraction of the available improvement.

Multiple Adoption Paths

This transformation does not need to happen in one sequence or everywhere at once. Most enterprises will blend several adoption motions depending on product maturity, regulatory exposure, platform strength, and leadership appetite.

Practice-Led Adoption

Keep the existing org chart and improve practices first: AI-assisted estimation, better specs, review heuristics, quality gates, and discovery experiments. This is the lowest-friction entry point for teams that want quick wins without reorgs.

Lifecycle-Led Adoption

Adopt AI one slice of the process at a time. Some teams start in discovery with prototypes and customer testing; others start in delivery with coding agents and harnesses; others begin in release and operations with CI/CD, ticket triage, and incident analysis.

Topology-Led Adoption

When shared-service bottlenecks are the main problem, start with team design. Stand up a small platform team, create an enabling team for coaching, and narrow stream-aligned ownership. This path is slower to launch but often produces the biggest system-level gains.

Risk-Led Adoption

Use the four big product risks as the organizing logic. If value is uncertain, invest in prototypes. If usability is weak, add design research. If feasibility is the issue, run engineering spikes. If viability is unclear, bring in finance, legal, security, and go-to-market early.

Practical Guidance

A realistic enterprise pattern is to pilot multiple paths at once: one stream-aligned team adopts AI in delivery, one product trio uses AI for discovery, one platform team builds shared guardrails, and one regulated area keeps more ceremony while selectively automating lower-risk work. The goal is not uniformity; it is learning where each model fits.

The motor was better; the architecture was still optimized for the old power model. His argument is that many enterprises are doing the same thing with AI now: dropping a new capability into workflows, approval chains, and planning cadences designed for a fully human system instead of rethinking the operating model itself.

Anti-Pattern 1

Bolting AI Onto Broken Processes

Over-Delegating to AI

Some teams go too far—letting AI write user stories, create retro actions, and suggest sprint goals without human review. Anti-Pattern 5

Pilot Purgatory

IDC research found that 88% of AI POCs don't make the cut to widescale deployment—for every 33 POCs launched, only four graduated to production. The path forward requires clear business goals, sufficient data quality, and investment in AI-fluent talent. (IDC/CIO.com)

Anti-Pattern 6

Only Measuring Coding Productivity

Organizations that evaluate AI impact only on coding tasks miss the bigger picture. When coding is less than half of a story's effort, gains from code generation alone don't translate to proportional project acceleration. Measure the full lifecycle.

A Product-Risk Lens Across the Pillars

SVPG's four big risks provide a useful cross-cutting lens for this entire research synthesis. They should shape how teams plan, staff, prototype, govern, and measure AI-augmented delivery.

Value Risk

Do customers actually want this? AI makes it cheaper to prototype, test messaging, and simulate scenarios, so teams should reduce value risk before scaling delivery. This strengthens the planning, discovery, and roadmap pillars.

Usability Risk

Can users figure out how to use it? AI-generated software can ship quickly but still confuse customers. Designers and product teams need faster prototype loops, walkthroughs, and usability checks woven into the engineering discipline and release process.

Feasibility Risk

Can we build and operate it well with our architecture, skills, and constraints? This is where harness engineering, platform investments, Team Topologies, and architectural guardrails matter most. AI reduces effort, but it does not erase technical complexity.

Viability Risk

Does the solution work for the business? Compliance, security, cost-to-serve, legal exposure, support burden, and go-to-market readiness should be addressed earlier. In regulated enterprises, AI can accelerate the work, but it also makes viability checks more important, not less.

How to Use This

For each significant initiative, ask four explicit questions before committing major capacity: how are we testing value, how are we validating usability, what is our feasibility evidence, and who is signing off on viability? That simple discipline prevents AI from speeding up the wrong bets.

10.2 The Evolved Product Spec

The traditional PRD is a long document written for humans, handed off to a design team, then handed off again to engineering. In this model it is replaced by an Agent-Ready Spec — a structured, living artifact primarily consumed by agents, with a working code prototype as its centerpiece. The spec and the prototype evolve together in the same feedback loop.

1 — Problem Brief

One paragraph, written by the PE. What problem are we solving, for whom, at what severity, and why now. This is the strategic anchor everything else derives from. Agents use it to weight their confidence scoring and surface relevant signals.

2 — Job-to-be-Done + Success Metric

Structured: When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]. Paired with a single measurable success metric — baseline and target — that agents will monitor post-ship. If no metric can be defined, the item is blocked from moving forward.

3 — Living Prototype

A working code prototype deployed to a preview URL. Generated by the Design Agent from the user flow spec using the product’s actual component library. This IS the design artifact — there is no Figma step. PE reviews it in the browser, leaves structured feedback, and the agent iterates. The prototype URL is embedded in the spec and updated on each revision.

4 — Risk Matrix

Agent-maintained, PE-reviewed. Four rows (Value, Usability, Feasibility, Viability), each with a confidence score (0–100%), supporting evidence links, and a list of open assumptions ranked by risk. Items cannot move to “ready to build” until all four domains cross their confidence threshold.

5 — Acceptance Criteria

Structured Given/When/Then format. Agents generate these from the user flow and prototype; PE approves. They map directly to test cases the Quality Agent generates — no manual translation. Out-of-scope items are listed explicitly to prevent agent over-implementation.

6 — Agent Context Notes

Short, PE-written notes that provide strategic context agents cannot infer from the spec alone. Examples: “compliance angle is the wedge, not productivity”, “enterprise buyers care about audit trails more than power users”, “acceptable to sacrifice mobile experience in v1.” Agents treat these as high-weight constraints on all decisions they make for this item.

Why This Format Matters

The spec is not a document for humans to read — it is an instruction set for agents to execute and a checkpoint surface for the PE to review. Every field is structured enough for agents to parse and act on, but short enough for a human to read in under 5 minutes. The prototype replaces the wireframe, the hi-fi mockup, and the “design review” ceremony in one artifact. The risk matrix replaces the weekly status meeting. The acceptance criteria replace the “definition of done” negotiation. The spec is the contract between the PE and the ensemble.

Intent Outlasts Code

Bret Taylor observed that the intent document — the written rationale for why something was built — has a longer productive lifespan than the implementation itself. Code gets refactored, rewritten, or replaced within months; the strategic intent remains interpretable for years and is what new engineers (human or agent) need most. This directly motivates the Agent-Ready Spec’s design: it is structured around the “why” — the Problem Brief, the JTBD, the Agent Context Notes — rather than the “how.” When agents load context to make implementation decisions, they are reading intent, not implementation specifications. The intent file is durable; the code it produced is replaceable.

10.2B Six Agentic Recipes for Rethinking the Value Chain new 🔗

The most interesting implementations are not “AI does task X faster.” They are workflows where the artifact itself becomes secondary and the system is designed around decision quality, speed of learning, and compounding policy capture. These recipes show how that looks across the PDLC.

1 — Signal to JTBD Brief

Trigger: Tickets, call transcripts, NPS comments, and session replays begin clustering around the same friction.
Agent move: Research agents extract the struggling moment, draft the JTBD, identify competing workarounds, and score signal strength.
Rethink: Discovery becomes continuous sensing, not a periodic research project.
Human value: Confirm whether this is strategically important enough to start a new learning loop.

2 — Opportunity to Investment Gate

Trigger: A new problem brief clears the “interesting” threshold.
Agent move: Value, feasibility, and viability agents run a mini diligence pack: willingness-to-pay, support burden, legal exposure, and architecture constraints.
Rethink: Prioritization becomes portfolio underwriting, not feature ranking.
Human value: Decide whether to fund more evidence, run a prototype, or kill the item early.

3 — Prototype Swarm

Trigger: The team needs to learn which interaction model helps the user complete the job with least friction.
Agent move: Design agents generate multiple coded variants, score them for cognitive load and accessibility, then route only the meaningful differences to review.
Rethink: Design becomes an evaluator-optimizer loop over working software.
Human value: Choose the principle and trade-off, not the first mockup.

4 — Feasibility Fork in Sandboxes

Trigger: Two or more implementation paths appear viable but imply different platform futures.
Agent move: A manager agent delegates parallel spikes into isolated sandboxes, each with estimates, failure modes, and migration implications.
Rethink: Architecture review becomes controlled comparative experimentation, not debate from first principles.
Human value: Pick the path whose upgrade story best fits strategy, cost, and timing.

5 — Release Sentinel

Trigger: A feature is technically complete but operational risk is non-trivial.
Agent move: Quality, release, and observability agents run canary release, anomaly detection, support-readiness, and rollback checks against encoded thresholds.
Rethink: Release management becomes continuous evaluation at the edge of production.
Human value: Own the thresholds and approve only the ambiguous or high-blast decisions.

6 — Escalation to Policy

Trigger: The same class of escalation appears repeatedly: legal ambiguity, pricing edge case, architectural conflict, or rollout uncertainty.
Agent move: The system runs RCA and converts the answer into one of three assets: a new skill, a new integration, or a permanent human gate.
Rethink: Management work becomes compounding system design, not case-by-case arbitration.
Human value: Turn judgment into reusable institutional memory.

Why These Recipes Matter

Each recipe replaces a handoff-heavy chain of documents, meetings, and interpretation with a smaller number of higher-quality human decisions. That is the deeper transformation: not merely automation of steps, but recomposition of the value chain around sensing, delegation, evaluation, escalation, and learning.

10.5 The Escalation → RCA Flywheel

Every escalation is a signal, not just a decision. After resolution, an RCA identifies whether a new skill, tool integration, or policy entry would prevent the same class of escalation from recurring. This is the primary mechanism by which agent autonomy compounds over time.

Missing Skill

The agent lacked a capability to resolve the question autonomously. Action: design and build a new skill with the required tool integration. Example: no willingness-to-pay scan meant every pricing question escalated.

Missing Data / Integration

The skill existed but could not access the data it needed. Action: add the tool integration or data pipeline. Example: the compliance agent had no access to the current legal knowledge base.

Missing Policy or Heuristic

The agent had data but no decision rule. Action: encode the resolved decision as a policy in the agent’s knowledge base. Example: which rollout percentage triggers automatic rollback.

Permanent Human Gate

The decision genuinely requires human values, political judgment, or external authority. Action: record as a permanent gate — agents pre-package all context but humans always decide. Example: any pricing change above X% ARR impact.

10.6 The Product Engineer’s Review Practice

The PE’s day is primarily structured review, not execution. With 13 agent clusters generating artifacts continuously across a live backlog, the review system must be rigorous: review everything at the wrong depth and the PE becomes a bottleneck; review too little and critical decisions get made without adequate judgment. The framework has three components: what to review, when it surfaces, and how to give feedback agents can act on.

What to Review — Three Tiers

Confidence score movements across the backlog · Emerging signal patterns from discovery agents · Skills and RCA report · Agent autonomy trend (escalations per cycle, by type)

Pre-ship readiness review (all four risk domains at threshold) · Significant spec changes requested by agents · New prototype revision deployed and ready for walkthrough

How to Give Feedback Agents Can Act On

Feedback is the primary interface between the PE and the agent ensemble. Vague feedback produces vague iteration. Structured feedback becomes knowledge base entries that improve future agent decisions on similar artifacts.

Prototype feedback

Walk through the preview URL as a user. For each issue: note the specific step, what felt wrong, and the why behind it (mental model mismatch, missing context, too many steps, etc.). Avoid prescribing the solution — diagnose the problem. The agent proposes the fix and iterates. Good: “Step 3 assumes the user already knows what a workflow template is. First-time users have no context.” Not useful: “Make this clearer.”

Risk matrix feedback

Review confidence scores and flag any domain where the score feels overconfident given the evidence. Provide the counter-signal: a customer quote, a known constraint, a market observation. The agent updates the evidence base and recalculates. The PE’s subjective signal is a first-class input — it often surfaces context the agent cannot discover from data alone.

Escalation resolution

Always include: (1) the decision, (2) the reason in 2–3 sentences, and (3) a generalisation — “in similar cases where X, prefer Y because Z.” The generalisation is what prevents the same escalation type from recurring. It becomes a policy entry in the agent’s knowledge base. Without it, the escalation was resolved but not learned from.

Spec context notes

Write these when agents are making reasonable decisions that are nonetheless wrong for strategic reasons not visible in the data. Be explicit and brief. “Prioritise enterprise compliance angle over self-serve UX in every trade-off for this item.” These notes carry more weight than any signal the agents can discover autonomously. Update them when strategy changes.

10.7 How Product Evaluation Evolves

When prototypes are generated in minutes and deployed instantly, and when agents can monitor production behaviour continuously, the episodic “run a usability test” or “launch an A/B test” model gives way to something more continuous and more granular.

From Periodic Usability Tests to Continuous Usability Monitoring

Session replay analysis, accessibility audits, and cognitive load scoring run on every prototype revision and every production deploy — not as one-off research events. Issues are surfaced continuously. The usability test becomes a targeted tool for confirming a hypothesis about a specific interaction, not a general health check.

From A/B Tests to Continuous Multivariate Evaluation

Because generating a code variant takes minutes, the experiment surface expands dramatically. Instead of testing one variable against one control, agents can run structured variant sets across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Statistical significance is monitored in real time; losing variants are retired automatically. The PE’s role shifts from “design the test” to “define what winning means.”

From Heuristic Review to Automated Heuristic Scoring

UX heuristics (Nielsen’s 10, WCAG, cognitive load frameworks) are applied automatically to every prototype and shipped surface. The PE reviews the exceptions — cases where the heuristic score is poor but the strategic rationale for the trade-off might still hold. This inverts the default: good design is the baseline expectation, not the review outcome.

From Post-Launch Measurement to Pre-Launch Confidence

The risk matrix replaces the post-launch retrospective as the primary evaluation moment. By the time a feature ships, all four risk domains must have reached their confidence threshold — meaning evaluation happened during development, not after. Post-launch monitoring confirms or refines the confidence scores; it is no longer the primary source of truth about whether something worked.

The Compounding Effect

As agents accumulate evidence across experiments, prototypes, session replays, and production signals, their confidence scoring becomes better calibrated over time. Early in the system’s life, the PE will need to challenge scores frequently. Over time, the agent’s confidence scores become reliable enough that the PE can trust Tier 2 spot checks to catch real surprises rather than needing Tier 1 deep reviews on every item. This is the second compounding flywheel, alongside the escalation → RCA loop.

Process, Not Just Person

John Collison argued that the majority of AI productivity gains are realised at the process level, not the individual level — and that organisations capture an order of magnitude more value when they redesign workflows around AI than when they layer tools onto existing ones. The measure of success is not how fast the PE works — it is how much the agent ensemble can handle without human input, and how precisely the human’s judgment improves the system when it is applied. Section 11 — Speculative Idea

Agent Architecture Deep Dive: Skills, Cycles & Shared Memory

How the current definition of an agent — LLM + memory + planning + skills + loops — evolves to support the Product Engineer model. And how a shared, evolving knowledge base across all agents becomes the system’s compounding asset.

11.1 The Agent Definition, Unpacked

The canonical definition of an AI agent is: a reasoning model (LLM) with persistent memory, planning and reflection capability, a set of callable tools/skills, and a loop that lets it observe results and iterate. Each of these five components needs to evolve significantly for the product engineering model to work at the fidelity described in Section 10.

LLM Core

The reasoning engine. Interprets domain signals, plans skill invocation sequences, evaluates evidence quality, and generates structured outputs (confidence scores, escalation packages, spec updates).

  • Domain-specialized system prompt
  • Structured output schema per artifact type
  • Calibrated uncertainty expression
  • Cross-agent communication format

Memory (multi-layer)

Not a single context window. Four distinct layers, each with different scope and durability.

  • Working: current task + recent outputs
  • Episodic: agent’s own past items and outcomes
  • Semantic: shared product/org knowledge base
  • Procedural: skill definitions + execution patterns

Planning & Reflection

The meta-cognitive layer. Decides which skills to invoke in what order, evaluates whether outputs are good enough, and determines when to escalate vs. iterate.

  • Confidence gap analysis: what would raise my score?
  • Skill sequencing: what to invoke next?
  • Self-evaluation: is this output trustworthy?
  • Stuck detection: N iterations, no improvement → escalate

Skills & Tools

Domain-specific callable capabilities that extend the LLM beyond reasoning into action — fetching real data, running analysis, generating artifacts, triggering deployments.

  • Each skill = a parameterized capability with defined inputs/outputs
  • Skills compose: output of one becomes input of another
  • Skills connect to external systems via tool integrations
  • Skill completeness = agent autonomy ceiling

Loops

The iteration mechanism. Each agent runs in domain-specific cycles driven by confidence thresholds, not task completion.

  • Observe: read current item state + memory
  • Plan: identify highest-value next skill to invoke
  • Act: execute skill, update working memory
  • Reflect: did confidence improve? What’s the next gap?
  • Gate: if threshold met → surface to PE; if stuck → escalate
The Key Evolution

The version of this definition that supports the product engineering model differs from the 2024 single-agent paradigm in three ways: (1) memory is multi-layer and persistent, not just in-context; (2) loops are confidence-threshold-driven, not task-completion-driven; (3) agents operate as an ensemble sharing a semantic memory layer — they are not isolated. These three differences are what make autonomous, continuous backlog grooming possible rather than just task execution.

11.2 Skills Augmenting Agent Cycles

Skills do not fire once and stop. They are invoked at specific points within each agent’s cycle, their outputs feed planning decisions, and results accumulate in memory to inform future cycles. Below are the representative cycles for six key agents, showing exactly which skills are invoked at each step and what memory is read or written.

Step 1 — Sense

Continuous signal ingestion

Skills invoked: signal_aggregation (runs on a scheduled trigger, pulling from Intercom, Zendesk, Gong, FullStory, Klue). Outputs: tagged signal stream written to working memory. Memory read: product KB for existing problem areas to check for signal reinforcement vs. novel patterns. Memory written: signal stream appended to episodic memory with timestamp and source weights.

Step 2 — Cluster

Pattern formation

Skill invoked: problem_clustering. LLM reasoning over current signal stream + historical clusters from episodic memory. Planning question: “Do new signals reinforce an existing cluster or constitute a new one?” Memory read: episodic (past clusters and their outcomes — did items created from them prove valuable?). Reflection: clusters with high historical false-positive rate are down-weighted.

Step 3 — Score

Opportunity ranking

Skill invoked: opportunity_scoring. Memory read: product KB (current strategy, active roadmap themes, prior experiment results in this problem area). Output: scored opportunity with confidence, supporting signal count, and strategic fit score. Reflection: “Is my confidence in this opportunity high enough to draft a backlog item, or do I need more signal?” If score below threshold, agent continues monitoring without creating an item.

Step 4 — Draft

Backlog item creation

Skill invoked: intake_structuring. Uses the opportunity score + clustered signals to auto-populate the Agent-Ready Spec template: problem brief, initial JTBD hypothesis, risk matrix (all four domains at initial low confidence), empty prototype slot, and assumption registry seeded from the signal evidence. Memory written: new item ID added to episodic memory with creation context. Orchestrator notified: item enters active grooming queue.

Step 5 — Monitor & Reflect

Continuous reinforcement loop

No new skills invoked. Agent watches for new signals that would update the item’s evidence base. Planning question: “Has anything materially changed since I last scored this opportunity?” If a new signal cluster contradicts the original, the agent triggers a re-score. Reflection cycle: after item is shipped and outcome data is available, agent reads the outcome against its original prediction and writes calibration update to episodic memory — improving future scoring accuracy.

Step 1 — Receive & Assess

Assumption triage

Triggered when an item enters active grooming. Memory read: product KB for any prior research in this problem area (past interview syntheses, experiment results, competitive data). Skill invoked: assumption_registry (seeds the registry from the problem brief and signals). Planning question: “Which assumptions have the highest risk × uncertainty product? Which skill can most efficiently reduce them?” Outputs a ranked skill invocation plan for this item.

Step 2 — Validate

Evidence gathering loop

Agent works through the ranked assumption list. For each high-priority assumption, invokes the matching skill: interview_synthesis (if prior interviews exist), experiment_design (if a test needs to be created), competitive_deep_dive (if market evidence is needed), market_sizing (if opportunity scale is unknown). Each skill output is evaluated: does this raise or lower my Value confidence score? Evidence is appended to the risk matrix and assumption registry is updated. Loop continues until either the confidence threshold is reached or the agent detects it is stuck.

Step 3 — Experiment

Active validation

If passive evidence (existing interviews, competitive data) cannot raise confidence to threshold, the agent invokes experiment_design to create a validation experiment (fake-door, concierge, or prototype test). Experiment spec is written to the item. After experiment runs (triggered externally or by the Design Agent deploying a prototype), experiment_analysis is invoked. Memory written: experiment results added to product KB under the relevant problem area — future items in the same area benefit from this evidence.

Step 4 — Stuck Detection & Escalation

Knowing when to stop iterating

Reflection trigger: if Value confidence has not improved by more than N points after M skill invocations, the agent packages an escalation. Package includes: current confidence score, assumption registry with open items highlighted, evidence gathered so far, and three possible resolutions (with trade-offs). Memory written: escalation type logged to episodic memory. After PE resolves, the rationale is written to the product KB as a policy entry: “For items in [problem class], [decision] because [rationale].”

Step 5 — Continuous Monitoring

Post-ship validation loop

After the item ships, Research Agent re-activates. Skill invoked: experiment_analysis against post-ship product analytics data. Does the observed outcome match the predicted outcome that justified building this? Memory written: prediction vs. outcome delta written to episodic memory and product KB. This closes the learning loop and improves future Value confidence calibration across all items in this problem area.

Step 1 — Receive

Spec intake and context loading

Triggered when Value confidence reaches threshold and item enters design phase. Memory read: product KB (design system tokens, component library API, existing patterns for this problem area, prior usability findings for similar flows). Working memory loaded with: problem brief, JTBD, success metric, cognitive load threshold for this product area. Planning question: “Are there prior flows for this type of feature I should adapt rather than generate from scratch?”

Step 2 — Flow & Score

Structure before pixels

Skill invoked: user_flow_generation (produces Mermaid diagram + narrated walkthrough). Immediately followed by: cognitive_load_scoring (scores the draft flow against threshold). pattern_matching (checks whether each step has an established UX pattern). Reflection: “Is the cognitive load score acceptable? Are any steps pattern-less and high-risk?” If score too high, agent revises the flow structure before generating the prototype — fixing logic, not pixels.

Step 3 — Generate & Validate

Code prototype loop

Skill invoked: code_prototype_generation (generates working code against the approved flow, using actual component library and design tokens). Immediately followed by: design_system_compliance (lints prototype code against component rules), accessibility_audit (runs Axe-core against deployed preview). Any violations are addressed autonomously if they have clear fixes. Prototype is deployed to a preview URL and written into the spec. Memory written: prototype version history added to item’s episodic memory.

Step 4 — Surface & Iterate

PE review loop

Orchestrator surfaces the prototype to PE as a Tier 2 spot check. PE reviews the preview URL and provides structured feedback. Design Agent receives feedback, maps each point to a specific flow step or component, and invokes code_prototype_generation again with the feedback as high-weight context. Cognitive load and compliance checks re-run on each iteration. Memory written: each feedback item + agent resolution logged to product KB under design patterns — building a growing library of “PE preferred this over that for these reasons.”

Step 5 — Usability Verification

Participant validation

Once PE approves the prototype, skill invoked: usability_test_script (creates test against the preview URL, distributes to participants via Maze, synthesises results). If findings surface mental model mismatches, agent returns to Step 2 (flow revision). If findings are within acceptable severity range, Usability confidence score is updated and item advances. Memory written: usability findings written to product KB under the relevant user segment and flow type — all future items targeting the same segment start with this knowledge.

Blast radius assessment

Triggered when design is approved. Memory read: product KB (architecture decision records, prior spikes in this area, known tech debt hotspots). Skills invoked in parallel: codebase_complexity_scan (maps affected modules, coupling risk), dependency_impact (maps downstream service effects), api_capability_map (identifies gaps between what the design requires and what current APIs provide). Planning question: “What is the highest-uncertainty technical question? Does it need a spike before design?”

Step 2 — Design & Estimate

Architecture proposal

Skills invoked: system_design_draft (proposes approach with trade-off summary), api_design (drafts API spec if new surface), data_model_design (proposes schema changes), effort_estimator (p50/p85 based on complexity score + velocity history). security_review runs against the proposed design. Reflection: “Are there two valid architectural approaches with materially different trade-offs? If yes, package both and escalate for PE direction.” Memory written: ADR draft added to product KB for this item.

Step 3 — Implement in Tasks

Decomposition and execution loop

Planning: agent decomposes approved design into implementation tasks ordered by dependency. For each task: feature_implementation invoked (generates code from spec + codebase context). After each task: code_review invoked (checks correctness, security, unnecessary complexity, pattern conformance). Violations are addressed autonomously. Memory read: product KB for coding conventions, prior similar implementations, and security policies. This loop runs until all tasks are complete or a blocker is found.

Step 4 — Debug & Refine

Error resolution loop

When tests fail or code review flags a pattern violation: debugging invoked (error + context → root cause → targeted fix). Agent attempts autonomous resolution for issues within its competence boundary. Reflection: “Is this the same root cause class as a previous issue on this item or in this codebase area?” Memory read: episodic (prior debugging sessions in this module). If the same issue class has recurred N times, agent writes it to the product KB as a known anti-pattern for this codebase area.

Step 5 — Handoff & Document

Knowledge capture

When implementation is complete: api_docs_generation invoked (generates OpenAPI docs from code). ADR is finalised with the chosen approach and rationale. Memory written: implementation decisions, patterns used, and any novel solutions added to product KB. Feasibility confidence score updated. Quality Agent triggered. Engineering Agent enters monitoring mode: watches for post-ship issues in the modules it touched, invoking debugging if new errors are attributed to those modules.

Step 1 — Triggered

Coverage baseline

Triggered by Engineering Agent task completion signal. Memory read: product KB (quality thresholds for this product area, prior test patterns for similar features, known flaky test signatures to avoid). Skill invoked: test_coverage_audit (reports current coverage in changed modules, identifies gaps). Planning: “Which coverage gaps are most likely to hide defects in this specific change?” Generates prioritised test generation plan rather than exhaustive coverage for its own sake.

Step 2 — Generate & Run

Test creation loop

Skill invoked: test_generation for each gap in the priority plan (unit, integration, and e2e coverage). Tests are generated from: the acceptance criteria in the spec, the user flow (for e2e scenarios), and edge cases surfaced by the security review. regression_scan runs the full suite. Any failures trigger the Engineering Agent’s debug loop. Reflection: “Are my generated tests actually testing the right thing, or are they testing the implementation rather than the behaviour?”

Step 3 — Non-Functional Checks

Performance and security

Skills invoked in parallel: performance_test (load test new endpoints against baseline), security_scan (dynamic scan of changed surfaces). Any finding above threshold goes to Engineering Agent for remediation. Performance regressions above threshold trigger a Tier 2 spot check for PE: “We can ship with this regression if you approve the trade-off, or hold while Engineering Agent investigates.” Memory written: performance baseline for this surface updated in product KB.

Step 4 — Release Preparation

Ship readiness

Skills invoked: feature_flag_management (creates flag for phased rollout), rollout_strategy (designs % rollout plan based on risk level from all four confidence scores), changelog_generation (writes release notes from PR history and spec). user_facing_docs invoked if the feature has customer-facing documentation implications. Release checklist generated. Memory written: rollout strategy written to product KB so future similar items start from this template.

Step 5 — Post-Ship Monitoring

Continuous verification loop

After ship: rollback_trigger monitors the feature flag cohort for anomalies. Observability Agent is notified to watch specific metrics for this feature. If anomaly detected within the rollout window, Quality Agent evaluates severity and either expands rollout (all metrics green), holds expansion (ambiguous signals), or triggers rollback (clear regression). Memory written: rollout outcome written to product KB — improving rollout strategy recommendations for future items with similar risk profiles.

Continuous — Route & Track

The meta-loop

The Orchestrator runs a continuous loop across all active items, not a sequential cycle. Every N minutes: reads the state of all items (confidence scores, which domain agents are active, which are stuck). Detects items that have changed state and routes work to the appropriate domain agent. Aggregates confidence scores into a per-item readiness score. No skills of its own are invoked; it reads agent outputs and makes routing decisions based on rules and LLM reasoning over item state.

Continuous — Conflict Detection

Cross-domain disagreement resolution

Monitors for cross-agent conflicts: Value score high + Feasibility score low, Usability approved + Accessibility audit failing, Design approved + Security review blocking. When detected: Orchestrator generates a conflict summary with both agent positions, the specific disagreement, and the options for resolution. Routes to PE as a cross-domain conflict escalation (different from a domain escalation — this requires a trade-off judgment across domains, not just domain expertise). Memory read: product KB for prior similar conflicts and how they were resolved.

Continuous — Escalation Queue

PE interface management

Receives escalation signals from all domain agents. De-duplicates (if two agents escalate the same root issue), prioritises by urgency and impact, and packages each escalation with: agent identity, type, current confidence, what the agent tried, what information would resolve it, and suggested options. Morning queue assembled from previous 24h escalations. Real-time alerts for immediate-tier escalations. Memory read: product KB for context relevant to each escalation. Memory written: after PE resolves, Orchestrator writes the resolution and generalisation back to product KB as a policy entry.

Continuous — KB Maintenance

Knowledge base stewardship

Orchestrator is responsible for the health of the shared product KB. Runs periodic consistency checks: Are any policies contradicting each other? Are any KB entries based on experiments that have since been superseded? Are any design patterns in the KB deprecated by a newer component in the design system? Flags inconsistencies to PE via the weekly digest. Memory written: KB entries updated, deprecated, or merged. This is the “garbage collection agent” equivalent for the knowledge layer, not just the codebase.

11.3 The Shared Memory Architecture

The most underappreciated part of the model. Each agent has its own working and episodic memory, but all agents within a product share a semantic memory layer — the Product Knowledge Base. And products within an organisation share a higher layer — the Org Knowledge Base. These shared layers are what make the system smarter over time, not just faster.

Working Memory Per agent · Per task · Ephemeral

Current backlog item spec, recent tool outputs, active reasoning chain, in-flight skill results. Cleared when the task cycle completes. Equivalent to the context window of an individual agent session. Nothing here persists.

Episodic Memory Per agent · Persistent · Domain-scoped

History of past items this specific agent processed: what skills were invoked, what confidence scores were achieved, what escalations were raised and how they resolved, and crucially — what its predictions were and whether they proved accurate post-ship. This is what enables per-agent calibration improvement over time. The Value Agent gets better at scoring because it has a record of being right or wrong.

Product Knowledge Base — Shared Semantic Memory All agents · Persistent · Product-scoped

The living intelligence of the product itself. All agents read from and write to this layer. It compounds over time as every decision, experiment, usability finding, and escalation resolution adds structured knowledge.

Customer & Market
Personas, JTBD map, experiment outcomes, interview synthesis, competitive intelligence
Design & UX
Pattern library, usability findings by segment, cognitive load baselines, PE design preferences
Engineering
ADRs, known anti-patterns, module complexity history, performance baselines, tech debt registry
Decisions & Policies
Escalation resolutions + generalisations, trade-off precedents, permanent human gates
Strategy
Current roadmap themes, PE context notes, priority signals, what has been tried and abandoned
Business
Revenue impact predictions vs. actuals, pricing decisions, legal policies, vendor risk registry
Org Knowledge Base — Cross-Product Semantic Memory All products · Persistent · Org-scoped

Patterns and learnings that apply across products. Shared design system, org-wide compliance and legal constraints, engineering standards and platform conventions, cross-product market intelligence, and generalised policies from escalations that have been promoted to org-level rules. Individual product KBs contribute to this layer when a learning is assessed as cross-product relevant. New product engineers — human or agent — can bootstrap from this layer rather than starting from zero.

Procedural Memory — The Skill Definitions Themselves System-level · Updated via skills development

The skill library: parameterized capability definitions, tool integrations, execution patterns, and composition rules. This layer evolves via the RCA → new skill development loop. It is the only memory layer that requires engineering work to update (all others are written by agents at runtime). Over time, procedural memory becomes the organisation’s primary product development IP — the distillation of everything learned into reusable capabilities.

Real-World Skill Taxonomy — Lessons from Building Claude Code

Anthropic’s own experience building and deploying skills for Claude Code provides concrete evidence for the skill architecture described above. After cataloguing all internal skills, Thariq Shafi (Anthropic) identified nine recurring categories that production skills cluster into:

1. Library & API Reference
How to correctly use a library, CLI, or SDK — including reference snippets and a gotchas list that pushes the agent beyond its default behaviour.
2. Product Verification
How to test or verify that code is working — paired with tools like Playwright, video recording of output, and programmatic assertions at each step.
3. Data Fetching & Analysis
Connecting to data and monitoring stacks — credentials, dashboard IDs, common query patterns, and analysis workflows.
4. Business Process & Team Automation
Automating repetitive workflows into one command — standup aggregators, ticket creation, weekly recaps stored in log files.
5. Code Scaffolding & Templates
Generating framework boilerplate for a specific function — combining instructions with scripts for services, migrations, and pre-configured settings.
6. Code Quality & Review
Enforcing code quality inside an org — deterministic scripts, style enforcement, and adversarial review processes.
7. CI/CD & Deployment
Fetching, pushing, and deploying code — PR monitoring, service deployment with rollout strategies, and conflict resolution.
8. Runbooks
Taking a symptom, walking through a multi-tool investigation, and producing a structured report — mapping symptoms to tools for debugging.
9. Infrastructure Operations
Routine maintenance and operational procedures — orphan resource cleanup, dependency management, and cost investigation.

The most actionable design insights from Anthropic’s experience: the highest-signal content in any skill is the Gotchas section — updated from real failure points, not generic advice. Don’t state the obvious — focus on information that pushes the agent out of its default behaviour. Use progressive disclosure via the file system: tell the agent what files exist and let it read them at appropriate times rather than loading everything upfront. Store helper scripts and libraries so the agent spends its turns on composition rather than rebuilding boilerplate. And critically: the description field is not a summary — it’s a trigger condition that determines when the skill activates. These lessons from a production-scale skill system directly validate the procedural memory architecture above: skills are living, composable, filesystem-native assets that compound organisational capability — not static prompts.

The Repository Is the Memory — A Practitioner Insight

Bret Taylor noted that the most effective agent knowledge base he encountered in practice was not a vector database or a purpose-built retrieval system — it was a directory of well-organised markdown files committed to a repository alongside the code. The rationale is concrete: a repo of markdown files is version-controlled (the KB has a history that agents and humans can traverse), diffable (changes are reviewable like code), human-readable (the PE can edit it directly without tooling), and natively loadable into the agent’s context window without a retrieval step. The sophistication lies in what is written and how it is organised — not in the retrieval mechanism. This is the implementation model for the Product KB and Org KB described above. Taylor also noted this resolves what he sees as a fundamental limitation of MCP (Model Context Protocol): rather than defining a protocol for agents to query data sources on demand, the more robust approach is to pre-load comprehensive structured context so agents have everything they need before they begin reasoning — not just what they think to ask for. The missing context problem is worse than the too-much-context problem.

11.4 The Shared KB as a Compounding Organisational Asset

The shared KB is the mechanism by which the system gets smarter over time, not just faster. Each agent interaction either reads from the KB (starting from accumulated intelligence) or writes to it (contributing new intelligence). The density and quality of the KB directly determines agent autonomy — a richer KB means more situations are covered by existing policies, patterns, and evidence, leaving fewer gaps that require escalation.

Every Experiment Is Preserved

Not just the outcome (“we shipped it”) but the full learning: hypothesis, methodology, result, and what it tells us about this customer segment or problem area. Future items in the same area begin with this evidence rather than starting from zero. Over time, the KB accumulates a rich map of what has been tried, what worked, and at what confidence level.

Every Escalation Becomes a Policy

Each resolution contributes a generalisation. After enough resolutions in a domain, entire classes of decisions that once required human judgment can be handled autonomously. The rate of novel escalations declines. The PE’s attention migrates from resolving known-class decisions to handling genuinely new situations — which is where human judgment is actually irreplaceable.

Every Design Decision Is Preserved

PE feedback on prototypes, trade-off directions, preferred patterns — all written back to the KB as design preferences and precedents. The Design Agent does not ask the same question twice. Future prototypes start closer to the PE’s aesthetic and strategic preferences without requiring a new review cycle for already-resolved questions.

Onboarding Collapses

A new Product Engineer (human or agent) joining this system reads the product KB and immediately has access to: every strategic decision made, every customer insight gathered, every technical trade-off resolved, every design preference established. Months of onboarding becomes hours. The institutional knowledge is no longer in people’s heads — it is in the KB.

Cross-Product Learning

When a pattern is discovered in one product — a pricing structure that reduces churn, a flow that reduces cognitive load, a codebase pattern that reduces tech debt accumulation — and it is assessed as cross-product relevant, it is promoted to the Org KB. Every subsequent product agent ensemble starts with this knowledge. The organisation’s product development capability compounds across products, not just within them.

The KB Is a First-Class Product

The KB is not a side effect of building products — it is itself an organisational asset requiring active stewardship. The Orchestrator runs KB consistency checks. The PE reviews KB health in the weekly digest. RCAs produce KB improvements, not just resolved decisions. Maintaining KB quality is as important as maintaining code quality. A degraded KB degrades agent autonomy just as technical debt degrades engineering velocity.

The Harness Is a Product Surface

Bret Taylor argued that the most strategically important product surface for enterprise software companies over the next decade will not be their user interface — it will be their agent harness: the structured context, KB, and skill definitions that determine how effectively AI agents can operate within the platform. Companies that invest in harness quality (documentation density, KB completeness, skill breadth, context richness) will dramatically outperform those that treat agent access as an API feature. This reframes the shared KB from an internal tool to a competitive asset. How well an organisation has codified its knowledge — decisions, patterns, customer insights, policies — directly determines how capable its agent ensemble can become.

The Architecture in One Sentence

Domain-specialized agents with confidence-driven loops invoke parameterized skills against real tooling, read from and write to a shared layered knowledge base, and surface only the residual judgment gaps to a human Product Engineer — creating a system where autonomy, accuracy, and institutional intelligence all compound continuously over time.
